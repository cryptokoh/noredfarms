The Complete Guide to Botanical Extraction Methods

A comprehensive series exploring every major technique for extracting bioactive compounds from plants — from ancient water infusions to cutting-edge supercritical CO2 systems. Each method shapes the chemistry, potency, and character of the final product.

Why Extraction Methods Matter

When you purchase a botanical extract, the extraction method determines what compounds end up in the final product. Different solvents and techniques pull out different molecules, meaning two extracts from the same plant can have very different chemical profiles, potencies, and applications.

Understanding extraction methods helps you make informed decisions about which products suit your needs, evaluate vendor claims, and appreciate the science behind the botanicals you use. This series covers nine distinct extraction techniques, from the simplest traditional methods to the most advanced industrial processes.

How to Use This Series

Each article below covers a single extraction method in depth: how it works, what equipment is involved, which plants and compounds it targets, what finished products it creates, and the practical advantages and limitations. Start with the methods most relevant to the products you use, or read the full series for a complete education.

Solvent-Based Extraction

These methods use a liquid solvent — CO2, ethanol, or water — to dissolve and carry target compounds out of plant material. The solvent determines which molecules are extracted based on their polarity and solubility.

Supercritical CO2 Extraction

Carbon dioxide under precise pressure and temperature becomes a tunable solvent that produces clean, solvent-free extracts. The gold standard for high-purity botanical oils and isolates.

Energy-Assisted Extraction

These modern techniques use physical energy — ultrasound, microwaves, or enzymes — to break open plant cells and accelerate the release of bioactive compounds, often combined with a solvent.

Ultrasonic-Assisted Extraction

High-frequency sound waves create microscopic cavitation bubbles that rupture plant cell walls, dramatically increasing extraction speed and yield while using lower temperatures.

Mechanical & Thermal Extraction

These methods rely on physical force or heat rather than chemical solvents. Cold pressing uses mechanical pressure; steam distillation uses heat and vapor to carry volatile compounds.

Cold Press Extraction

Pure mechanical force expels oils from seeds and plant material without heat or solvents. The method of choice for carrier oils, seed oils, and cold-pressed essential oils.

Choosing the Right Method

No single extraction method is universally superior. The best choice depends on the plant material, the target compounds, the intended product form, and practical constraints like cost and scale. Here are the key decision factors:

  • Target compound polarity: Water extracts polar molecules, CO2 and cold press target non-polar compounds, ethanol covers a broad range.
  • Heat sensitivity: For delicate compounds, cold press, enzymatic, and low-temperature CO2 methods preserve integrity. Steam distillation and microwave methods involve heat that can degrade some molecules.
  • Scale and cost: Water and ethanol extraction are accessible at any scale. CO2 requires significant capital investment. Ultrasonic and microwave methods fall in the middle.
  • Product form: Essential oils require steam distillation. Tinctures need ethanol. Full-spectrum oils often use CO2. Teas and decoctions are water extracts by definition.
  • Regulatory requirements: Solvent-free methods (CO2, water, cold press, steam) avoid residual solvent testing requirements.
